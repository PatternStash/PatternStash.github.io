How DMC Floss is Represented on Computer Screens


DMC, or any other floss for that matter, is a real-life object. We see its color because certain wavelengths of light bounce off of it, hit our eyes, and then our brains interpret it. Computer screens are different: they emit specific wavelengths of light instead, rather than reflect it off of a surface. These differences can cause complications when designing cross stitch patterns on a computer which then are rendered in real-life thread. What you see on screen may, in other words, not match what you get when you stitch the pattern. Consider also the effect of thread being three-dimensional. The thread may be a certain color, but you're going to see different shades of it depending on how light hits the thread. A computer can only represent one shade at a time, most often using RGB values (that's R-ed, G-reen, and B-lue).

This is further complicated by different monitors representing the same colors slightly differently. Some monitors may be warmer in tone, and some colder. As an example, I have two screens, where one is definitely much warmer in tone, so I make sure to always work on one screen rather than both when eyballing colors.

How Do We Match DMC to RGB?


When designing cross stitch patterns, the designer will want to make sure that the colors represented on screen are as close to the real-life threads as possible. However the patterns I was making didn't seem to line up exactly (perfectionist streak...!) with the DMC threads when you looked at the graphics vs. the actual threads. To be sure, I made a little experiment. My hypothesis was that the software I use for designing patterns uses a palette that wasn't very good at representing DMC floss colors accurately - they felt pale and washed out. So to test this, here's what I did:

  1. I found two photographs of the DMC floss palette and for each DMC color (yes, 482 of them; twice!), I calculated the average single color using the software Inkscape.
  2. I extracted the palettes from three popular cross stitch design software packages (one of them manually; that's another set of 482 measurements!).
  3. I also found two web sites with DMC palettes available in RGB format, and I extracted these too, for comparison.

What I Learned


Not a single one of the palettes I sampled matched any of the other palettes. The three software packages all came out different, the two palettes used on the websites were also different, and worse, had hues that were simply wrong. The two sampled photographs yielded different results too, as compared both to each other and the palettes in the software packages.

Regarding the photographs of the DMC palettes, one of them, when compared to the other, was at a much lower resolution and came out as highly saturated. The other, photoed at a high resolution, came out closer to the existing software palettes, but still with enough variation to be noticably different. This second DMC photograph, however, appears to be taken in good neutral or natural lighting (or to have been scanned) and the resulting colors seems to better match actual thread. I have, as a result from this, decided to work, from now on, with a palette based on these photographs.

When matching colors between a photograph or graphic to make a pattern out of, eyeballing is your enemy. Given that some monitors represent colors differently, if you observe a color by eye, and then match it up against a thread in a software's DMC palette, also by eye, then you're introducing the possibility of error based on the color temperature of your screen. Instead, match colors using the RGB value of the observed color (in your source graphic/picture) to the RGB value of the thread. These values are agnostic of your screen, and rely on a purely mathematical comparison.
